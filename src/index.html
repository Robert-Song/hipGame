About the game

Hip is a game described by Martin Gardner in Problem 39 of My Best Mathematical & Logic Puzzles, a collection from compilations of his Scientific American column, Mathematical Games. In this game, two players - hipsters, if you will - take turns placing markers on a checkboard, determined not to form squares with their markers (squares being such a drag, this game having been named in the 1960s). Squares include those formed by equal diagonals, etc., which can make seeing the danger of one's moves - or the error of the other player's moves - rather difficult.

As such, the puzzle asks only about the possibility of the game coming to a draw, as a sort of logic exercise, but with the computer checking for - and marking - squares, it's playable and enjoyable as any tabletop game!. Playing above makes it very clear what one means by squares formed on equal diagonals.

On this website, the player has the choice to play against any of three different AIs - the first is a hard-coded strategy that estimates and updates the safety of each square; the second, a traditional recursive look-ahead AI (available in varying depths); the third, a clueless player marking randomly with no sense not to form a square. The latter is recommended for first-time players.

Co-developed by Robert Rattray and Robert Song.
